Laurel Schwulst challenges the view that websites are mere tools or static pages. She demonstrates that they can be creative, personal, and always evolving. What stood out most to me is her idea that a website is like a living thing that grows, changes, and reflects the person behind it. Rather than seeing websites as strictly professional or perfect, she suggests we should view them as flexible and expressive, similar to a room, garden, or even a puddle. This made me realize that websites don't need to stick to strict rules; they can be experimental and personal. I was particularly intrigued by her point that the internet consists of individuals, not just large companies.
She notes that while social media platforms are run by corporations, personal websites allow people more freedom and control over their identity and content. This caused me to rethink my internet use, since having my own website gives me the ability to create my own space rather than depending on platforms. Overall, her insights show that websites are not just technical tools; they are creative spaces that can influence both the web and our personal identities. Question: If websites are meant to be personal and always changing, how do we balance creativity with keeping them clear and usable?

Olia Lialina changes how we view old websites by showing that platforms like Geocities were more than just poorly designed or outdated pages. They played a significant role in internet history and culture. She explains that Geocities allowed regular people to create their own homepages, making the web feel personal, creative, and community-driven. What struck me most was her comparison of these old websites to “ruins.”
Even when they are broken, have missing images, or contain dead links, they still convey a story about the past and how people engaged with the internet. I found her description of the shutdown of Geocities particularly interesting. Much was lost during that process. While some pages were saved, many were incomplete and felt like abandoned spaces due to missing images or links. The examples in the reading, such as pages labeled “under construction” or “this page has moved” (as shown in the screenshots), demonstrate how websites were constantly changing but also easily forgotten. This realization made me understand that the internet doesn’t last forever, and much personal history can vanish if not preserved. In summary, the reading illustrates that old websites are not worthless or embarrassing. Instead, they are valuable records of creativity and everyday life online. They help us see how the internet once felt more personal and less dominated by large platforms. Question: How is today’s internet, with social media and big platforms, different from the more personal and creative web that Geocities represented?

I agree with Olson’s claim that any artwork produced after the web has been influenced by it in some way. What convinced me in the reading is that she’s not saying all art is about the internet, or that it has to exist online to count as post-internet. Instead, she frames the internet as a condition we’re already inside of. Even artworks that are physical, handmade, or intentionally offline are still shaped by the web through how they’re documented, circulated, and encountered. We now expect to see art on screens before we ever see it in person, and that expectation changes how artists think about form, scale, and visibility. Olson’s point is less about technology and more about awareness. Artists are making work knowing that images will be copied, shared, compressed, and recontextualized. In that sense, it’s hard to imagine any contemporary artwork being completely untouched by the web, because the web has become part of the cultural environment in which art exists, whether artists embrace it or resist it.

I agree with Coklyat and Finnegan’s view that alt-text is not just a technical tool for accessibility; it is also a creative and political practice. What stood out to me is how they shift the perception of alt-text from being invisible and functional to something expressive and intentional. They demonstrate that description is never neutral. Every decision about what to include or highlight shows a perspective. By framing alt-text as poetry, they challenge the notion that accessibility is less important than the “real” artwork. I found their argument that writing alt-text is a form of care and responsibility especially convincing. It requires slowing down and focusing, instead of assuming sight is the default way to understand images. Accessibility becomes part of the artwork itself, not an afterthought. The reading ultimately reshapes access as something positive, expanding participation and changing how we engage with digital spaces.

Ramsey Nasser challenges the notion that computers are neutral, universal tools by demonstrating how they are shaped by language and culture. What struck me most is his point that even initiatives like One Laptop Per Child, which aim to empower children worldwide, still depend on programming languages and systems rooted in English. The bias exists not only in visible interface choices but also in the underlying code, libraries, and protocols that require English keywords and naming conventions. His experiment with the Arabic programming language shows how hard it is to break free from this structure, since modern programming relies on shared libraries and systems that cannot easily be renamed or translated. Ultimately, Nasser reframes programming as a social and historical practice rather than just a technical one. He shows that the toughest issues in computing are cultural, not computational.
I was particularly impacted by his discussion of naming as a cultural act. Names in programming are not neutral labels; they carry history, power, and linguistic assumptions. Because programmers have to use the exact names defined in existing libraries and protocols, English acts as a gatekeeper for participation in software development. This makes the concept of a truly “personal computer for children of all cultures” seem nearly impossible under current conditions, as full participation still requires English literacy. This reading encourages me to view technology not as an objective framework but as something created from layers of human choices that reflect specific cultures and exclude others. Questions: 1. If programming is fundamentally collaborative and relies on shared naming systems, can we redesign computing to avoid favoring one dominant language? 2. How should global tech companies address the language biases in existing protocols and libraries if rewriting them is practically unfeasible?

3/24

Silvio Lorusso questions the view that design is entirely a creative and controlled practice. He demonstrates how it is increasingly influenced by systems, templates, and automation. What stood out to me most is his argument that much of today’s design is already “pre-made” through default settings and formats built into software. Designers do not always start from scratch; they often work within systems that already make decisions about layout, style, and structure. This makes design feel less like pure creativity and more like assembling or adjusting what already exists. Lorusso ultimately shows that design is not just about individual skill but also about navigating and interpreting larger technological systems.
I found his idea that “form follows format” especially interesting. This means the tools and platforms we use shape the final design more than we might realize. Templates, presets, and automated systems speed up and simplify design work, but they also create sameness and limit originality. This relates to his point about semi-automation. Designers are not completely replaced by machines; instead, they work alongside systems that reduce their role to small adjustments or repetitive tasks. It made me rethink what being a “designer” means today, since the role can range from creative decision-maker to someone simply organizing prebuilt elements. Overall, the reading shows that design is becoming less about making and more about managing systems and understanding their cultural impact. If design is increasingly controlled by templates and automated systems, can designers still maintain true creative authorship? And how can designers push back against the sameness created by default formats while still using the tools that make their work efficient?

What stood out to me in this reading is how Vera Frenkel’s "String Games," explores what gets lost through early technology. The piece uses telecommunication and performance to illustrate how connection across screens can still feel incomplete, as if something is always missing. I found it intriguing how the idea of a “poetics of absence” appears, suggesting that memory and meaning are shaped just as much by what isn’t there as by what is. It also made me think about how today’s digital media still creates that same gap between presence and reality. Overall, the reading urges us to question the belief that technology completely connects and shows that it can reveal absence and loss. Questions: 1. How does Frenkel’s idea of “absence” change the way we think about digital connection today? 2. Do you think modern social media reduces or increases this sense of “missing presence” that the reading discusses?

What stood out to me in this reading is how Paul Soulellis transforms something as simple as “posting” into a bigger idea about making something public. He connects the act of posting to physical actions, like nailing a notice to a wall, where publishing demanded attention. I found it interesting how he contrasts that with today’s “feed,” which flows continuously and blends into the background of our daily lives. This shift makes publishing feel less like a single event and more like an ongoing performance that never really ends. I was especially struck by his idea that feeds create a kind of “new boredom,” where people watch things not because they are meaningful but because others are watching too. It made me think about how often I scroll without truly paying attention, yet still feel engaged just by being part of that shared experience. The reading also points out how feeds mix human and non-human voices, like algorithms and bots, making it harder to tell what is real or intentional. Overall, Soulellis shows that publishing today focuses less on clear authorship and meaning and more on endless flow, participation, and systems that we don’t fully see or understand. In summary, the reading argues that we are shifting from a world of fixed, intentional publishing to one with continuous, algorithm-driven feeds that shape how we consume and interpret information. It also suggests we need new ways to pay attention and question what we see, especially as these systems become more invisible and harder to control. Questions: 1. If feeds are designed to keep us watching continuously, how can we become more aware of when we are passively consuming versus actively engaging? 2. How does the presence of algorithms and bots in the feed change our ability to trust what we are seeing online?

4/14

This reading argues that the internet began as something creative and open, but has since become dominated by large platforms that focus on profit and attention rather than genuine connections. The author compares this to other technologies, like cars, which start as useful tools but ultimately change society in harmful ways. I found the idea of “convivial tools” significant. It highlights how technology should encourage creativity, independence, and ownership instead of controlling users. The call for personal websites resonated with me as a way to reclaim control and make the internet feel personal again. Overall, it prompted me to reconsider how much of our online activity is shaped by platforms rather than by ourselves.
Discussion Questions: 1. Do you think it’s realistic for people to shift away from large platforms and return to personal websites? Why or why not? 2. How can we balance convenience, like social media, with the need for ownership and creativity online?

4/28

Groys argues that the Internet fundamentally changes how we understand art by turning it from something fictional and separate into something that exists as real, visible information. What stood out to me most is his idea that art online becomes more about documentation and process than a finished product, since everything is constantly accessible and connected to the artist’s real identity. This also connects to his point about surveillance, where the Internet tracks and records everything, meaning artists are no longer creating in private but are always being watched and interpreted as data. I think this is interesting because while the Internet seems more open and accessible, it also makes it harder for art to feel meaningful or distinct when it exists alongside everything else. Overall, the reading made me think about whether the Internet actually expands artistic freedom or quietly limits it in new ways.
Discussion Questions: If art on the Internet is more about documentation than the final product, does that change what we consider “real” art? Does the constant visibility and surveillance of the Internet limit creativity, or does it just change how creativity is expressed?
